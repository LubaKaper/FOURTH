Dr. Chen,

In March 2023, Mount Sinai signed onto the AIM Postpartum Hemorrhage safety bundle across all five birthing facilities. That was a public commitment, and it was the right one.

The gap I am looking at
Q3 2025 CMS data shows the system's severe maternal morbidity rate at 198 per 10,000 deliveries, the fourth quartile statewide, roughly 34% above the academic-center peer average. The bundle is in place. The outcome trend is moving the wrong direction.

I read your team's August 2025 statement attributing part of this to acuity mix from referral patterns, and I think that is a fair point that does not get enough room in these conversations. I am not writing to suggest the bundle is failing.

I am writing because the bundle ends at discharge, and the women showing up in those Q3 numbers are the ones the bundle was meant to protect. The 12 weeks after delivery are where the protective effect either compounds or fades, and right now Mount Sinai's commitment effectively ends at the curb.

Twenty minutes. I will bring the peer benchmarks. You tell me whether closing that gap is something the institution is ready to take on, or whether the conversation is premature.

Sources, so you can pressure-test before we talk:
, AIM bundle participation registry (saferbirth.org)
, CMS maternal morbidity Q3 2025 (data.cms.gov)
, Mount Sinai equity report, Sept 2024

SMM rate: 198 per 10,000 deliveries (Q3 2025). Academic-center peer mean: 148 per 10,000. NY state quartile: 4th. PPH bundle implementation: March 2023, system-wide. Trend over the last four quarters: +7.6% net. Acuity mix adjustment per your August 2025 statement: noted, not yet quantified in the public data.

Two things I would want to know if I were in your seat. First, of the SMM cases driving the Q3 number, what is the postpartum-period split, events at delivery versus events between discharge and 6 weeks? In our cohort across 31 academic centers, the post-discharge share has been climbing as delivery-period bundles mature. Second, has the team modeled what fraction of the trend is acuity-mix versus what is genuinely happening to your patients in the fourth trimester?

The reason I ask: when we work with Quality teams at peer institutions, the AIM bundle data tells us delivery-period care is getting safer. The post-discharge data tells us we are losing ground on a different surface. Different problem, different intervention.

Twenty minutes if useful. I will bring the cohort breakdown. No pitch, no deck, your numbers, your read.

Robert,

New York's 12-month postpartum Medicaid extension changed the math on fourth-trimester care, and looking at Mount Sinai's Q3 2025 numbers, I wanted to put the financial frame in front of you before someone else does.

The financial picture, drawn from public data
Mount Sinai delivers ~3,847 babies per year. 30-day postpartum readmission sitting at 7.8% (national academic median: 6.1%). At an average DRG-weighted readmit cost of ~$13,400, the 1.7-point gap above peer median translates to roughly $876K in avoidable readmission cost annually, and under the NY 12-month extension, the Medicaid coverage window now overlaps the SMM events, which means the system is absorbing more of that cost.

The CMS Birthing-Friendly designation Mount Sinai earned in 2024 is real, and I am not here to discount it. The designation measures structure. Readmission and SMM measure outcomes. Right now those two are pulling in opposite directions, and the financial exposure sits with you.

Two questions worth a 20-minute conversation:

First, what is the current attribution of postpartum readmits in the system's value-based contracts, pure FFS, shared savings, or downside risk? The answer changes the ROI math by an order of magnitude.

Second, has Revenue Cycle modeled the 12-month Medicaid extension's effect on the postpartum loss ratio? Most CFOs I talk to have not seen that number isolated yet.

Happy to bring our cost-of-readmission model and walk through it. No pitch attached.

Sources:
, KFF Medicaid postpartum coverage tracker (NY)
, CMS Hospital Compare readmission data
, Mount Sinai CMS Birthing-Friendly designation
